# Filing Strategies Guide

## Filing Status Options

### For US Citizens and Green Card Holders

| Status | Who Qualifies | When It's Best |
|--------|---------------|---------------|
| **Single** | Unmarried, or legally separated | Default for unmarried filers |
| **Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)** | Married as of Dec 31 | Usually best for married couples — wider brackets, higher deduction |
| **Married Filing Separately (MFS)** | Married, filing individually | When one spouse has high medical expenses, student loans (IBR), or liability concerns |
| **Head of Household (HOH)** | Unmarried with qualifying dependent | Better brackets and higher standard deduction than Single |
| **Qualifying Surviving Spouse** | Widowed within 2 years with dependent | Uses MFJ brackets and deduction |

### For Non-US Citizens on Visas

Visa holders must first determine **tax residency** via the Substantial Presence Test (SPT):

```
Current year days + (Prior year days ÷ 3) + (2 years ago days ÷ 6) >= 183 → Resident Alien
```

**Exempt individuals** (F-1/J-1 students in first 5 calendar years, diplomats, etc.) exclude their days from the SPT count.

| SPT Result | Filing Options |
|------------|---------------|
| Resident Alien | Same as US citizens (1040) |
| Nonresident Alien (NRA) | 1040-NR — Single or MFS only |
| NRA, married | Can elect RA under §6013(g) → must file MFJ |

## MFJ vs MFS Decision

MFJ is better in most cases. Consider MFS when:

| Situation | Why MFS May Help |
|-----------|-----------------|
| One spouse has high medical expenses | 7.5% AGI floor is lower with lower individual AGI |
| Income-driven student loan repayment | Payments based on individual AGI, not combined |
| One spouse has tax debt or liability | Protect the other spouse from collections |
| Community property state complications | May simplify allocation |
| One spouse is NRA without §6013(g) election | Can't file MFJ without election |

**MFS penalties**: Lower SALT cap ($20K vs $40K), some credits disallowed, lower income phase-out thresholds.

## NRA vs RA (§6013(g)) Decision

For NRAs married to another NRA or RA, the §6013(g) election trades treaty benefits for full RA deductions:

| Feature | NRA (1040-NR) | RA via §6013(g) (1040 MFJ) |
|---------|---------------|----------------------------|
| Mortgage interest deduction | **Not available** | Available |
| Standard deduction | Not available | Available |
| Treaty exemptions | Available | Not available |
| Investment income (FDAP) | Flat rate or treaty rate | Graduated rates |
| NIIT (3.8%) | Exempt | Subject to |
| Education credits | Not available | Available (if not phased out) |
| Child tax credit | Limited | Full |
| Worldwide income taxable | No (US-source only) | Yes |

**Rule of thumb**: If you have a mortgage, RA MFJ almost always wins — the mortgage interest deduction typically exceeds treaty benefits.

## Standard vs Itemized Deduction

### 2025 Standard Deduction

| Status | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| Single | $15,000 |
| MFJ | $30,000 |
| MFS | $15,000 |
| HOH | $22,500 |
| Additional (65+ or blind) | +$1,600 single / +$1,300 married |

### When to Itemize

Itemize when total itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction. Common tipping points:

- Mortgage interest > ~$15K (single) or ~$20K (MFJ)
- High state/local taxes (SALT) in CA, NY, NJ, etc.
- Large charitable contributions
- Significant medical expenses (>7.5% of AGI)

### MFS Itemize Rule

If one MFS spouse itemizes, the other **must also itemize** (cannot take standard deduction).
